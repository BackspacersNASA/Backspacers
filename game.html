When an asteroid strikes, it generates a massive shockwave that propagates through the Earth's crust; if the impact occurs in the ocean, the initial blast will displace a colossal amount of water, generating a tsunami.

The tremendous energy unleashed by meteorite impacts is often compared to the power of potent explosive detonations-- the initial impact alone can produce seismic shocks far more powerful than conventional earthquakes. The severity of these disasters is directly proportional to the size and velocity of the impacting asteroid.

When a meteorite enters Earth's atmosphere, it undergoes a process called "ablation," where intense friction causes it to shed microscopic particles and dust. This phenomenon provides a unique opportunity for scientific research. Scientists use advanced tools like LIDAR to analyze these dust particles and radar to track the plasma trails left behind. These methods allow them to measure crucial atmospheric data such as temperature, density, and wind speeds in the upper atmosphere, a region otherwise difficult to study due to its unsuitability for satellites.

NASA's Spaceguard program functions as a planetary early-warning system with the primary mission of discovering, cataloging, and tracking near-Earth objects (NEOs) like asteroids that could pose a threat. To accomplish this, NASA utilizes a network of ground-based telescopes that image sections of the sky. By comparing these images, specialized computers can identify the faint movement of an NEO. This detection allows astronomers to ultimately determine if it presents any danger to Earth.

A Hypervelocity Asteroid Intercept Vehicle uses a kinetic impactor to create a crater on an asteroid, followed by a nuclear detonation inside the crater to maximize disruption. This method aims to scatter most fragments, reducing the risk of Earth impacts from debris. The HAIV system is designed to counter threats with warning times of under 10 years. Left as a critical emergency defense option, ongoing studies continue to refine it.

The Double Asteroid Redirection Test (DART) was a NASA mission designed to evaluate a method of planetary defense against near-Earth objects by using the kinetic impactor technique, which involves colliding a spacecraft with a celestial body to alter its orbital path. Its success validated this model as a viable strategy for mitigating asteroid threats. The mission targeted Dimorphos, a small moonlet of the asteroid Didymos. It is effective against such relatively small celestial bodies. This method of planetary defense depends on early detection.

The probability of an asteroid impact increases as it approaches Earth. If an asteroid passes within 36,000 km, it enters the ring of geostationary satellites crucial for global communications and weather data. The likelihood of impact, although small, is regarded with the highest level of concern by planetary defense organizations globally.
